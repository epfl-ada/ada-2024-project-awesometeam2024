By sticking with box office revenue, we're focusing on financial success, but it's important to keep in mind there's more than one way to measure a movie's impact.

Timing is Everything

As we can imagine, the release date as a strong impact on the film's performance. First by plotting the data, we discover that summer is the best period to release a movie. More specifically, in the month of June. This was confirmed when assessing the correlation between the release month and the revenue. A correlation of 0.072 suggests release season alone is not a strong predictor of revenue in our dataset.

This is obviously linked with the fact that people are more likely to go to the cinema during the summer : kids are on holiday and parents are looking for activities to do with them.

Too short, too long, or just right?

The runtime of a movie is a key factor in its success. Too short and the audience might feel cheated, too long and they might get bored. We have discovered that movies between 1920 and 1950, movies were shorter, probably for technical reasons. But when technique was no longer a problem, movies started to get longer and longer before shrinking again to stabillize around 105 minutes.

Dual interactive plot with selector for the year : 1 plot for average revenue, 1 plot for average runtime

But what about the revenue ? We analysed the average revenue for each runtime categories and found with confidence that movies considered as "long" compare to others tend to perform better than shorter movies.

Plot from the notebook with average box office revenue for each runtime categories for different periods.

Of course, you could say that this data does not represent the ground truth but only shows what happened in the past. And you are absolutly right ! We are not saying that short movies will perform less but simply that less and less movies are produced with a short runtime. One reason could be that those shorter movies are now streamed on different platforms like youtube.

Who Brings the Magic?

The Youth Advantage vs Timeless Talent

Looking into the data, it seems that films with younger casts seem to perform better on average, suggesting a potential trend that producers might want to explore further. Younger actors may connect better with certain target audiences, particularly with the youngest. However, while this observation is interesting, it's important to approach it with caution. The smaller sample size for younger casts makes the data less stable and more prone to volatility, meaning this trend could be more coincidental than reliable. In contrast, the larger quantity of samples for films featuring adult casts provides more robust statitics. It's a good place to remind you to be careful when analyzing data !

Epilogue

Great story isn't it ? Indeed, we had a lot of fun exploring the data and trying to understand the movie industry. But from this analysis, many aspects must still be discussed.

For example, the casting : it has the same biases through the years as our society. Hence, we cannot say that films with a predominance of male actors will continue to perform better since it was (still is) harder for women to lead roles in big movies. Movies with high ethnic diversity perform better but the ethnicities that lead to this result are mainly european ethnicities.

We also have to keep in mind that the data we used is not exhaustive. We only used the CMU dataset and we know that there are many other datasets that could have been used. We also know that the data is not perfect and that we had to clean it a lot before being able to use it.

Finally, we have to remember that the movie industry is an art industry and analysis of the past does not predict the futur. The last words are a remember that the data is not the truth and that we have to be careful when analyzing it.
